Positive & Negative Sentiments by Focus Area

Critical Minerals + Tariffs

Negatives

  • Chinese leverage over critical minerals: China’s dominant share of mining, refining, and new export controls gives it enduring leverage over rare earths and other inputs that nuclear-relevant systems require.
  • Chokepoints in antimony and key metals: Export cuts, price spikes, and strained Western stockpiles for antimony and other dual-use metals expose munitions and critical components to disruptive supply shocks.
  • Tariffs and long transition window: Tariffs, resource nationalism, and a decade-plus timeline to diversify away from China keep semiconductor and critical mineral supply chains volatile and vulnerable.

Positives

  • Allied minerals frameworks: Frameworks with Japan, Australia, Malaysia, Thailand, Central Asia, Brazil, the UAE and others explicitly target critical minerals for defense and clean energy, broadening trusted suppliers for the nuclear industrial base.
  • Domestic mining and processing buildout: New U.S. projects in antimony, rare earth separation and metallization, mine-to-magnet chains, and enabling infrastructure such as the Ambler Road are rebuilding critical capacity onshore.
  • Strategic finance and stockpiles: DoD, EXIM, Commerce, USGS-guided programs and private capital are funding stockpiles, magnet plants and rare earth projects that backstop defense and semiconductor supply chains.
  • Policy alignment on criticality: The expanded U.S. critical minerals list and targeted regulatory relief for mining and processing facilities align permitting and federal investment with long-term industrial base resilience.

Microelectronics

Negatives

  • Weak U.S. chip production base: U.S. fabs now produce only a small share of global semiconductors, keeping nuclear command-and-control and support systems exposed to foreign manufacturing shocks and geopolitical pressure.
  • Risks from export controls and decoupling: Tight export controls and shrinking access to the China market could erode U.S. chipmakers’ R&D budgets even as China races for AI chip self-sufficiency, fragmenting supply chains and raising costs.

Positives

  • High-performance computing expansion: DOE partnerships with AMD, Nvidia, and Oracle on new AI supercomputers significantly expand domestic modeling and simulation capacity that underpins nuclear analysis and design.
  • Advanced fabs and processes in the U.S.: Blackwell chip and wafer production in Arizona, Intel’s 18A process for Microsoft’s Maia 2, and GlobalFoundries’ GaN ramp in Vermont shift key logic and power devices into U.S. manufacturing.
  • Emerging tools and policy support: Novel X-ray lithography efforts, superconducting germanium research, and proposals like the GAIN AI Act all point toward more secure domestic chipmaking and priority access for national security missions.

CFIUS + Domestic Production

Negatives

  • Shutdown-driven CFIUS delays: The Hindalco–AluChem stall during the 2025 government shutdown shows how disruptions to CFIUS review can slow allied investment in upstream materials that feed semiconductor and industrial supply chains.

Positives

  • Domestic enrichment capacity: GLE’s successful large-scale SILEX enrichment test in North Carolina is a major step toward domestic low-enriched uranium supply and reduced reliance on foreign fuel.
  • Nuclear-grade additive manufacturing: The MANTRA hub collaboration on metal additive manufacturing for nuclear-grade materials can speed qualification of advanced parts and enable more secure, on-demand components for reactors and facilities.

Toxic Chemicals

Negatives

  • PFAS and TSCA enforcement pressure: EPA’s aggressive PFAS and TSCA agenda raises compliance costs and operational uncertainty for NIB-relevant facilities that depend on regulated chemicals in manufacturing and decontamination.
  • EU PFAS and SVHC tightening: New EU PFAS restrictions and SVHC listings increase reporting and redesign burdens for electronics and materials suppliers that support the nuclear industrial base, with potential delays and cost increases.
  • Fragmented chemical regimes: Diverging U.S. and EU approaches to toxic chemicals create regulatory whiplash that complicates cross-border sourcing and can store up future liability at defense-adjacent sites.

Positives

  • Risk-based PFAS carve-outs: The EU move to a more risk-based PFAS regime with long transition periods and sector derogations preserves some critical industrial uses while giving suppliers time to plan substitutions.
  • Support for data-center chemicals: TSCA prioritization and relief for certain data-center chemicals ease constraints on AI and high-performance computing buildout that nuclear programs increasingly rely on.
  • Improved chemical risk management: Sustained focus on PFAS and other high-risk substances is pushing firms to map chemical use and invest in mitigation, ultimately supporting a cleaner and more resilient industrial base.
